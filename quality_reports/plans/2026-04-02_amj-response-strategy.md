## Structural Diagnosis (My Read)

All 30+ individual reviewer comments collapse into **3 structural problems**:

### Problem 1: Wrong Theoretical Conversation
The paper argues against institutional voids and bricolage — but those aren't the right foils. There's a whole literature on entrepreneurship in authoritarian/repressive contexts that the paper ignores (Bradley & Klein 2016, Dickson 2003, Neuberger et al. 2023, Peng 2001, Puffer & McCarthy 2001). The reviewers can't evaluate the contribution because they don't know where the paper wants to sit. This also creates the "too many lanes" problem — without a clear home, the theory sprawls.

### Problem 2: Missing Inductive Legitimation
Co-optation reads as pre-decided, not inductively developed. Reviewers can't see the hermeneutic and analytical moves that went from archival texts to theoretical claims. Rick's transparency table is the right surgical fix. Without it, the paper looks like it's illustrating a concept rather than building theory.

### Problem 3: Causal Claims Exceed the Evidence
The strongest claim — a "recursive cycle of mutual capture" — is not what the data can show given the sample (5 prosecuted cases from 126). The data supports selective tolerance and pragmatic enforcement at the local level. The state incorporation claim in particular needs either stronger evidence or revised language. R2 is most emphatic here.

## Proposed Response Strategy

### The Three Required Moves

**Move 1: Re-anchor the theoretical conversation**
Pick one theoretical home and commit. The strongest option based on the evidence: the **authoritarian entrepreneurship / institutional constraints** literature (Bradley & Klein, Peng, Puffer & McCarthy, Neuberger et al.) with **inhabited institutions** as a complementary frame (DeJordy et al., R3's suggestion). This does several things simultaneously: answers R1-1a (most emphatic missing lit), answers AE-1, provides a clear foil for co-optation as a new concept, and gives the contributions something specific to extend. De Certeau should either be integrated into findings/discussion or removed — it's currently dead weight (R2-1c).

**Move 2: Build inductive legitimacy (Rick's table + analytical walkthrough)**
Add the data-to-theory transparency table Rick proposes. Columns: Archival material type → Source criticism move → Interpretive inference → Theoretical code/category. This directly addresses R1-2a, R1-2b, AE-7, AE-8, R3-4, and R2-1d. It is a surgical, high-leverage fix. Relatedly, the case-to-model movement needs explicit narration: how were the 5 cases coded? How did tactics emerge as categories?

**Move 3: Calibrate causal claims to the evidence**
This is the biggest strategic decision (see below). R2-3a is the most potentially fatal comment. Either: (a) revise language from "recursive mutual capture" to "selective tolerance + pragmatic incorporation," or (b) add supplementary evidence that local officials were incorporating ventures in a patterned (not just ad hoc) way. Option (a) is safer and faster; option (b) is stronger if data supports it.

## Key Strategic Decisions (Authors Must Decide)

These cannot be resolved by Claude — they require author judgment:

**Decision 1: The causal claim — revise down or defend?**
R2's core concern: "mutual capture" and "recursive cycle" are strong causal claims. Data shows selective tolerance, uneven enforcement, pragmatic ad hoc responses. Authors must choose:
- **Option A (Revise down):** Reframe as selective tolerance → pragmatic incorporation, drop "mutual capture" language, accept that the contribution is descriptive-process rather than causal. Lower bar, faster revision.
- **Option B (Defend + evidence):** Add evidence from the broader 126-case archive that state incorporation was patterned (not ad hoc). Requires more archival work but preserves the stronger theoretical claim.

**Decision 2: Which theoretical lane to commit to?**
- **Option A:** Authoritarian entrepreneurship literature as primary home. Co-optation extends this by showing how entrepreneurs actively shape state responses (not just adapt).
- **Option B:** Institutional entrepreneurship / inhabited institutions as primary home. Co-optation shows how invisible actors create institutional settlements.
- **Option C:** Hybrid — use authoritarian entrepreneurship as the entry and inhabited institutions as the theoretical contribution. This could be the strongest framing.

**Decision 3: The scope of generalization**
R2-4b: Contemporary analogies are wildly overextended. Do you want to (a) drop them entirely and keep the paper historically bounded, or (b) narrow to a specific structural feature of Maoist China that is theoretically portable (e.g., "systems where state is simultaneously prohibitor and sole resource reservoir")?
